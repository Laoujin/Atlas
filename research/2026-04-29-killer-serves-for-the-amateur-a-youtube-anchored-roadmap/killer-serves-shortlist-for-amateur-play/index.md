> **Decision.** Learn these three, in order:
> 1. **Short forehand pendulum** with a same-motion backspin / no-spin pair — your bread-and-butter setup serve [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/) [[12]](https://pingsunday.com/third-ball-attack-table-tennis/).
> 2. **Long fast no-spin** to the elbow / deep backhand — used 2–3 times a game it is "a free point about half the time" against sub-2000 players [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138).
> 3. **Short forehand tomahawk** to the receiver's forehand — different motion, draws errors and pop-ups at amateur level [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/).
>
> Stop there. Three serves with deep variation beat seven with no spin.

## Why these three

Every men's top-10 player opens with a pendulum motion [[2]](https://tabletennisteacher.com/serves/), and Larry Hodges names the forehand pendulum, reverse pendulum, forehand tomahawk and backhand sidespin as the four motions that win at every level [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144). Coach Ferenc Horvath's seven-serve curriculum (pendulum, reverse pendulum, backspin, no-spin, hook, tomahawk, backhand) is the realistic ceiling [[14]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/video/7-most-effective-table-tennis-serves/), but for an amateur trying to add 1–3 weapons, the marginal return collapses fast after the first three.

The shortlist is built around one principle from Racket Insight: *"the power of a serve doesn't lie in its spin, it lies in its deceptiveness"* [[4]](https://racketinsight.com/table-tennis/learn-serving/). Two of the three serves below share a motion (pendulum) so backspin, no-spin and the long fast variant all come from the same arm path. The tomahawk gives you a second motion family the opponent has to read separately.

## The shortlist

| # | Serve | Motion | Length & target | Spin | Why it kills at club level | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Short pendulum (backspin / no-spin pair)** | Forehand pendulum, same arm path; vary contact point on blade [[4]](https://racketinsight.com/table-tennis/learn-serving/) | Short, 2nd bounce near opponent's end-line, to forehand or middle [[11]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/rate-your-serve-how-strong-is-your-service-game/) | Heavy backspin OR near-zero (handle-side contact) [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/) | Sets up the canonical short-backspin → long-push → loop pattern [[12]](https://pingsunday.com/third-ball-attack-table-tennis/); the no-spin twin pops their push up for a kill [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/) | If you only ever serve backspin, opponents groove on it [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144) |
| 2 | **Long fast no-spin** | Same pendulum action, lower toss, longer first-bounce, contact near handle | Deep, low over net, into the playing elbow or wide deep backhand [[6]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/destroy-opponents-long-serves/) | None (a "float" ball) | "Free point about half the time against players rated under 2000" — push pops up, loop overshoots [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138). Lodziak: use long serves up to 80% vs weaker, defensive opponents [[7]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-often-should-you-use-long-serves/) | Useless once an opponent counter-loops cleanly — drop to ~2–3 per game then [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138) |
| 3 | **Short forehand tomahawk** | Elbow up, throw-the-axe motion, brush around the side of the ball [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/) | Short, into the opponent's forehand corner | Sidespin (kicks toward their wide forehand), variable backspin/topspin | Unfamiliar motion at club level; *"many players couldn't even return the ball"* [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/). Pairs with the pendulum so opponents face two motions to read | Less spin range than pendulum [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144); long tomahawks get punished by anyone who attacks |

## How to drill each one

### 1. Short pendulum, backspin ⇄ no-spin

Mechanics: shake-hand grip with index finger slid up onto the backhand rubber so the wrist is free; toss ≥16 cm; brush the bottom of the ball with the tip of the blade for backspin; **on the no-spin variant keep the same arm path but contact near the handle, where the blade is moving slowly** [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/) [[15]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-to-do-a-legal-table-tennis-serve/). First bounce close to your own end-line so the second bounce dies near theirs.

Why it works: the receiver reads "backspin" from the wrist snap and pushes; on the no-spin twin their push pops up [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/). This is the single highest-leverage serve at club level because the third-ball attack — short backspin → opponent's long push → forehand loop — is the most-used winning pattern in table tennis [[12]](https://pingsunday.com/third-ball-attack-table-tennis/).

Sequencing: serve heavy backspin twice, then the no-spin on the third. Watch their wrist — if they re-use the push motion, the point is yours.

### 2. Long fast no-spin to the elbow

Mechanics: same pendulum body action so it looks identical out of the hand. Lower toss → less time for the receiver to read. First bounce **near your own end-line** so the ball clears the net and lands deep [[6]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/destroy-opponents-long-serves/). Aim for the crossover point between the receiver's forehand and backhand (the "elbow") — this is where Hodges' free-point line comes from [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138).

Usage rhythm: two minds on this. Hodges says use it sparingly — 2–3 times per game to preserve surprise [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138). Lodziak says against passive pushers and choppers you can ride it up to 80% of your serves [[7]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-often-should-you-use-long-serves/). Reconcile by **testing once in game one** — if the opponent is comfortable, retreat to Hodges' 2–3-per-game cadence; if they panic, lean on it.

### 3. Short forehand tomahawk

Mechanics: stand square or slight forehand-side, **lift the elbow** so the forearm is roughly horizontal, then "throw the tomahawk" — flick the wrist around the side of the ball, brushing the upper-right (for a right-hander) and finishing under [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/). Keep it **short** — long tomahawks are the one cell where Hodges flags the serve as weak against attackers [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144).

Why it pays at amateur level: the motion is uncommon at club, so receivers misread the spin direction (the ball kicks the *opposite* way to a pendulum) [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/). Even Hodges, who downgrades it at advanced level, calls it a chaos generator at intermediate [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144).

Once both pendulum and tomahawk feel automatic, the upgrade path is the *reverse* of either — Larry Hodges: *"they might never become equal in effectiveness as the regular version, but at the very least they give you a variation that the opponent isn't used to"* [[10]](https://butterflyonline.com/reverse-serves/). The TableTennisDaily Academy 5-step drill (isolate wrist square-to-table → add forearm → add waist → vary contact point → consolidate) is the cleanest path in [[13]](https://tabletennisdailyacademy.com/5-steps-to-master-the-reverse-pendulum-serve/).

## What "killer" actually means at amateur level

Tom Lodziak's five elements give the audit list — spin, placement, variation, deception, and recovery into the third-ball stance [[11]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/rate-your-serve-how-strong-is-your-service-game/). Most club players over-index on spin and ignore the other four. The shortlist above is engineered to pay off all five:

- **Spin** — heavy backspin on serve 1 [[5]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/the-surprising-power-of-a-no-spin-serve/), sidespin on serve 3 [[9]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/).
- **Placement** — short to opponent's forehand (1, 3) ⚠ awkward over the table; long to the elbow (2) [[6]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/destroy-opponents-long-serves/).
- **Variation** — three serves cover all four major spin types and two motion families [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144).
- **Deception** — same-motion backspin/no-spin pair on serve 1; pendulum-vs-tomahawk motion swap forces a re-read [[4]](https://racketinsight.com/table-tennis/learn-serving/).
- **Recovery** — all three are forehand serves from a forehand-pivot stance, so the third-ball loop is the natural follow-up [[12]](https://pingsunday.com/third-ball-attack-table-tennis/).

## Pitfalls to dodge

| Pitfall | Why it kills the serve | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Hidden serve / no toss | Illegal; toss must be ≥16 cm, near-vertical, from a flat open palm, ball visible throughout [[15]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-to-do-a-legal-table-tennis-serve/) | Toss higher than you think; keep your free arm clear of the ball line |
| Short serves that bounce 3+ times | Telegraphs short, lets receiver step in early [[1]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144) | Aim for second bounce *just before* the end-line, not deep into the opponent's half |
| Long serve to the wide forehand | Easiest ball in table tennis to counter-loop | Long serves go to the elbow or deep backhand only [[8]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138) |
| Different motion for each spin | Removes deception, the actual win condition [[4]](https://racketinsight.com/table-tennis/learn-serving/) | Drill backspin and no-spin pendulum from a single arm path before adding any new serve |
| Practising alone but never timed | Service game decays under match pressure | 5–10 minutes of focused serve practice every session beats one long monthly session [[3]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/3-table-tennis-serves-every-player-should-master/) |
