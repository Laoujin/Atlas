The four angles converge on one prescription: an amateur should add **three serves, not seven**, anchor them in the **forehand pendulum motion family**, and treat every serve as a launcher for the third-ball loop rather than a point-winner in itself [[1]](https://pingsunday.com/third-ball-attack-table-tennis/) [[2]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/144). The shortlist — short pendulum with a same-motion backspin/no-spin pair, long fast no-spin to the elbow, and the short forehand tomahawk — is what the deception, tutorial, and drilling angles all happen to optimise for. That is not coincidence: the pair-from-one-motion is *also* deception lever #1 [[3]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/27), and the tomahawk supplies the second motion family the receiver must read separately [[4]](https://www.experttabletennis.com/tomahawk-serve/).

**The unifying principle is contact-point, not arm speed.** Heavy backspin comes from blade-tip contact on the fast end of the arc; the no-spin twin from contact near the handle on the slow end — same swing, same wrist snap [[3]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/27) [[5]](https://pingsunday.com/contact-point-max-spin-table-tennis-serve/). Every video pick, every solo drill, every match-play tactic in the children downstream of this insight ladders up to it. If you can't yet vary spin from a fixed motion, no amount of tutorial-watching will help.

**One contradiction worth flagging.** Long-serve frequency: Larry Hodges prescribes 2–3 per game to preserve surprise [[6]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138); Tom Lodziak says ride it up to 80% against passive pushers [[7]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-often-should-you-use-long-serves/). The reconciliation is tactical, not technical — test once early in the match and let the opponent's response set the cadence. Both coaches are right against their respective opponent classes.

**A unified six-to-eight-week schedule** falls out of the children:

- **Week 1.** Watch Lodziak/Bryant *4 killer serves* [[8]](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EuXKHxRcRbQ); start the daily 15-minute solo block with paper targets and 10-in-a-row [[9]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/improve-serves-solo-service-practice/).
- **Weeks 2–4.** Drill the same-motion backspin/no-spin pair with blade-tip vs handle contact, video-record once a week to verify toss height and arm path [[10]](https://tabletennisdailyacademy.com/video-record-improve-table-tennis/).
- **Weeks 4–6.** Add length disguise and the long fast no-spin; partner third-ball drills walking from fixed feeder return to fully random [[11]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/3rd-ball-attack-training-drills/).
- **Weeks 6–8.** Layer the tomahawk and a deliberate post-contact fake on the no-spin twin [[12]](http://blog.uberpong.com/adam-bobrow-trick-serve-timo-boll/).
- **Month 2+.** Only after 40% of points started with each serve end in a won third ball [[13]](https://topspin11.com/training/improvement-roadmap/) — add the reverse-pendulum upgrade via the TableTennisDaily Academy 5-step drill [[14]](https://tabletennisdailyacademy.com/5-steps-to-master-the-reverse-pendulum-serve/).

**Legality is a constant, low-frequency hazard.** The 16-cm vertical toss, visible contact, and free-arm clearance rules apply to every serve in the shortlist [[15]](https://www.tabletenniscoach.me.uk/how-to-do-a-legal-table-tennis-serve/), but post-contact theatrical follow-through remains legal under ITTF Law 2.10.1.7 — only deliberate second strikes are penalised [[16]](https://www.pingskills.com/table-tennis-forum/double-hit-legal-or-illegal). Hidden contact and a lingering free arm are the two fouls that quietly cost amateur players matches.

**The open question after all four angles ran:** the deception research notes that purely-after-contact fakes stop fooling receivers above ~2000 rating [[17]](https://www.pingskills.com/table-tennis-forum/fake-motions-on-serves), and the shortlist's frequency-rule advice flips at the same level [[6]](https://www.tabletenniscoaching.com/node/2138) — what does the next-tier roadmap look like for a player who has graduated past club opponents? That ceiling is unaddressed here.
